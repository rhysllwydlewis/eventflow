Accessibility at Events: Making Your Event Inclusive

Making your event accessible and inclusive ensures that every guest — regardless of mobility, hearing, vision, or other needs — can fully participate and enjoy the occasion.

📚 8 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 15 March 2026

⚖️ Why Accessibility Matters

Around one in five people in the UK lives with a disability or long-term health condition — a figure that rises significantly among older age groups. For a wedding or large event, it's likely that a meaningful proportion of your guest list will have some accessibility need, whether visible or not.

The Legal Framework

Under the Equality Act 2010, service providers — including venues — have a legal duty to make reasonable adjustments to avoid putting disabled people at a substantial disadvantage. As an event host, while you may not be directly responsible for the venue's legal obligations, choosing an inaccessible venue and failing to make reasonable adjustments can still mean that guests are excluded from your event.

For commercial events open to the public, organisers have additional duties around accessibility under the Act. Even for private events, inclusivity reflects the values of the hosts.

The Moral Case

Beyond legal requirements, there is a straightforward human argument: if you've invited someone to your event, you want them to be able to fully participate and enjoy it. An elderly grandparent who can't access the ceremony, or a deaf friend who misses the speeches, has been inadvertently excluded. A little planning removes these barriers entirely.

🏛️ Choosing an Accessible Venue

Accessibility should be a primary consideration when shortlisting venues — ideally before you fall in love with a location that turns out to have significant barriers.

Key Questions to Ask Every Venue

  • Step-free access: Are all event spaces accessible without steps? Where lifts exist, are they large enough for a standard wheelchair?
  • Accessible toilets: Are there accessible WCs near all event spaces, not just at one end of the building? Are they unlocked and well-maintained?
  • Parking: How many Blue Badge spaces are there? How close are they to the main entrance? Is the path from the car park level and well-surfaced?
  • Hearing loops: Are hearing loops installed in ceremony or reception spaces? Are staff trained to switch them on?
  • Lighting: Can lighting levels be adjusted? Very dim or flickering lighting causes issues for guests with visual impairments or epilepsy.
  • Seating flexibility: Can the layout be adapted to accommodate wheelchairs, mobility aids, or guests who need to be seated throughout?
  • Sensory environment: Can music volume be controlled? Is there a quieter space available for guests who need to step away from noise?

Visit in person: Always visit a venue with accessibility in mind. Read it from the perspective of someone in a wheelchair, or someone with a visual impairment. Walk the route from the car park to the ceremony space to the reception. What seems fine on a floor plan may not work in practice.

✉️ Communication and Invitations

Good accessibility planning starts at the invitation stage. The more you know about your guests' needs in advance, the better prepared you can be.

Asking About Accessibility Needs Sensitively

Include an accessibility question on your RSVP form. The simplest approach is an open-ended question: "Do you have any accessibility requirements we should be aware of?" or "Please let us know if you have any dietary requirements or accessibility needs." This is non-intrusive, normalises the question, and gives guests the space to share what's relevant without feeling singled out.

Avoid overly specific questions that might feel categorising ("Do you use a wheelchair?"). An open question collects the same information while respecting guests' privacy and dignity.

Accessible Invitations

  • Ensure invitation websites use sufficient colour contrast and are navigable by keyboard and screen reader
  • Offer a plain-text alternative to highly designed PDF invitations for guests with visual impairments
  • Use a minimum font size of 12pt in printed materials; 14pt or larger for guests you know have visual difficulties
  • Provide clear directions to the venue including accessibility details (accessible parking, step-free entrance location)

🤝 On-the-Day Support

Even the best planning can leave gaps. Having a designated person — a member of the wedding party or a coordinator — who is briefed on accessibility provisions and empowered to act on them is invaluable.

BSL Interpreters

If you have deaf guests attending, particularly for formal elements like the ceremony or speeches, consider booking a British Sign Language (BSL) interpreter. Book through an NRCPD-registered interpreter agency and provide them with a copy of any scripts, readings, or speech notes in advance. Position them where they are clearly visible to all deaf guests without blocking sightlines for others.

Seating Arrangements

  • Reserve aisle seats for guests who may need to leave and return easily
  • Designate wheelchair spaces that are genuinely integrated into the seating plan — not tucked away at the back or sides
  • Ensure guests with hearing difficulties are seated where they have clear sightlines to speakers and can benefit from any hearing loop
  • Consider a dedicated quiet table away from the speakers for guests with sensory sensitivities

Wayfinding

Clear, well-positioned signage helps all guests navigate the venue, but is particularly important for guests with cognitive or visual impairments. Ensure signage uses high-contrast text and simple, unambiguous language. Assign ushers or volunteers to key decision points (car park to entrance, entrance to ceremony room) to provide personal guidance.

🍽️ Dietary and Sensory Needs

Dietary requirements and sensory sensitivities often overlap with disability and health conditions. Managing them with care and discretion is part of inclusive event planning.

Dietary Requirements

Collect dietary information at the RSVP stage and pass a comprehensive list to your caterer at least four weeks before the event. Ensure the caterer can confidently manage serious allergens — particularly the 14 major allergens under UK food safety law. Guests with severe allergies should have their meal individually plated and clearly identified, not just offered an alternative at the buffet.

Label buffet items clearly with common allergen information. If in doubt, speak to guests personally to confirm what they can eat — they will appreciate the care taken.

Sensory Considerations

  • Keep at least one space at your event that is quieter and lower stimulation — useful for autistic guests, those with anxiety, guests with migraines, and parents with young children
  • Avoid or minimise the use of strobe lighting, smoke machines, or pyrotechnics without warning guests in advance
  • Strong perfumes and floral arrangements can trigger migraines or allergic reactions — don't over-scent the event space
  • Ensure there are somewhere to sit at all times — standing-only events exclude guests with mobility or fatigue conditions

💻 Digital Accessibility for Hybrid and Virtual Events

If your event has a virtual or hybrid element, digital accessibility becomes equally important.

  • Captions and subtitles: Enable live captions on video calls for deaf or hard-of-hearing participants. Most major platforms (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet) offer auto-captioning, with varying accuracy. For key moments, consider a human captioner.
  • Recording: Make a recording available afterwards so guests who experience technical difficulties or need to review content can do so at their own pace.
  • Accessible platform: Choose a platform with good screen reader support. Test it in advance with any guests who use assistive technology.
  • Simple joining instructions: Provide step-by-step instructions in plain language, with a contact number for technical support on the day.
  • Quiet participation options: Allow guests to participate via text chat if audio participation is a barrier.

📋 Checking In After the Event

Following up with guests after the event — particularly those with accessibility needs — is a thoughtful final step that many event hosts overlook. A brief message asking whether everything worked well for them, and whether there was anything you could have done better, shows genuine care and provides invaluable information for future events.

Most guests will appreciate being asked. Many will have navigated the day without complaint despite encountering difficulties; their feedback helps you and helps others who will host events in future.

Remember: Accessibility is not a tick-box exercise — it's an ongoing commitment to ensuring that everyone feels genuinely welcome and able to participate. The effort you put in will be noticed and appreciated by those who benefit from it most.
