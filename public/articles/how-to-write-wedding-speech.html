How to Write a Wedding Speech

Whether you're the best man, maid of honour, father of the bride, or a proud parent, writing a memorable wedding speech doesn't have to be daunting.

📚 7 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 13 March 2026

🎤 Understanding Your Role

Before you write a single word, it helps to understand what's expected of your specific speech. Different roles carry different conventions — and different audiences.

Father (or Parent) of the Bride or Groom

Traditionally the opening speech at the wedding breakfast. The tone is warm, proud, and reflective. You'll welcome guests, talk about your child, welcome their new partner into the family, and raise the first toast. This is the most sentimental of the speeches — don't be afraid to show emotion.

The Couple

Many couples now choose to speak together or individually. This is your chance to thank everyone who helped, acknowledge absent loved ones, and express what this day means to you. Keep it heartfelt, specific, and — above all — genuine.

Best Man

The best man speech is traditionally the most comedic, but "funny" doesn't mean cruel. The best man thanks the couple (on behalf of the bridesmaids, in the traditional format), tells stories about the groom, and wishes the couple well. The challenge is finding stories that are entertaining without being embarrassing — aim for affectionate teasing, not humiliation.

Maid of Honour

Not always included formally, but increasingly common. The maid of honour speaks about the bride with warmth and humour, often balancing the best man's groom-focused stories. This is a speech about friendship and love — lean into both.

📝 Structure of a Wedding Speech

Every good wedding speech follows a similar arc, regardless of the specific role. Think of it in three parts: open, middle, and close.

The Opening

Start with a confident, attention-grabbing line. A light joke, a surprising fact, or a warm opening observation all work well. Avoid starting with "For those who don't know me…" or "I looked up some quotes on the internet…" — both are clichés that signal a lack of originality.

Introduce yourself briefly — your name and your relationship to the couple — so that everyone in the room knows who you are before you continue.

The Stories and Substance

This is the heart of your speech. Choose two or three stories or observations that illustrate who the person (or couple) really is. Specific, vivid details make stories memorable — "she always arrived late but with the best snacks" is far better than "she's a really thoughtful person".

For a best man or maid of honour speech, one story should ideally show how the couple's relationship changed your friend for the better. This lands with warmth and gives the humour a meaningful context.

The Toast

End with a clear, warm toast. Signal it's coming ("So if you'd all raise your glasses…"), say something sincere and brief, then invite guests to drink. Don't trail off — land the ending with confidence and clarity.

💡 Finding the Right Stories and Anecdotes

The search for good material is often where people get stuck. Here's a practical approach:

  • Mine your memories: Think back to specific moments — the first time you met, a particular holiday, a crisis you navigated together, a habit that drives everyone mad but is secretly endearing.
  • Ask other friends: Different people will have different stories. A quick message to mutual friends often unearths gold you'd forgotten or never knew.
  • Look at photos and messages: Old group chats, photo albums, and shared memories can trigger specific, vivid stories you might otherwise have overlooked.
  • Choose stories that reflect well: The best stories reveal a person's character — their generosity, their silliness, their loyalty. Even gently embarrassing stories should ultimately show why this person is so well-loved.

A word of caution: Avoid stories involving exes, anything that could cause genuine embarrassment to the families, references to illegal activity, or anything the subject hasn't given implicit permission to share. If in doubt, leave it out.

😄 Getting the Tone Right

The most successful wedding speeches balance humour with genuine sentiment. Pure comedy without heart can feel hollow; pure sentiment without lightness can feel heavy. The sweet spot is warmth — stories and observations that make people laugh and feel moved, often at the same time.

Using Humour Well

Good wedding humour is observational, affectionate, and specific. "He once drove four hours to the wrong city for a football match" is funny because it's specific and shows character. Generic jokes downloaded from the internet rarely land as well as something personal.

Self-deprecating humour tends to land better than jokes at others' expense. If you make yourself the butt of the joke occasionally, it relaxes the room and shows confidence.

Sentiment Without Schmaltz

Genuine emotion is always more powerful than performed emotion. Speak from experience: "I've watched my brother become calmer, kinder, and happier since he met her, and that's all I ever wanted for him" will resonate far more than a borrowed quote about love.

⏱️ Length and Timing

Wedding speeches should be short enough to leave the audience wanting more. As a general guide:

  • Father of the bride/groom: 5–7 minutes
  • The couple: 5–8 minutes (combined), or 3–4 minutes each
  • Best man: 5–8 minutes
  • Maid of honour: 4–6 minutes

When speaking at a normal pace with pauses for laughter, 500 words takes roughly 3–4 minutes. A 750-word speech is approximately 5 minutes. Time yourself reading aloud — you'll almost always speak faster than you think under pressure, so practise at a slower pace than feels natural.

Three speeches of 6–8 minutes each is about right. Any more than that and guests begin to tire. If there are additional speakers, keep them to 2–3 minutes each.

🎯 Delivery Tips

A beautifully written speech can still fall flat if the delivery doesn't land. Here's how to give yourself the best possible chance.

Practise Out Loud

Reading silently is not the same as speaking aloud. Your speech will sound very different when performed. Practise at least five or six times, ideally in front of at least one other person who can give honest feedback.

Eye Contact

Speak to the room, not to your notes. Prepare your speech well enough that you can glance up regularly. Know where your key laughs are — and look up when you deliver them so you can enjoy the response.

Pace and Pauses

Nerves make most people speak too quickly. Deliberately slow yourself down, especially at the beginning. Embrace pauses — particularly after a punchline or a heartfelt moment. Silence is your friend; it gives the audience time to feel and react.

Managing Nerves

Almost everyone is nervous before a wedding speech, including experienced public speakers. The trick is to channel that nervous energy rather than fight it. Deep breathing before you stand up, a smile as you begin, and the knowledge that everyone in the room wants you to succeed — because they love the couple — will all help.

Practical tip: Print your speech in a large, readable font (at least 14pt) with double spacing so you can glance down and find your place easily. Number the pages clearly. Cards can be easier to handle than folded paper.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting with an apology — "I'm not very good at public speaking…" immediately lowers expectations and doesn't help anyone. Just begin.
  • Reading word-for-word without looking up — The audience wants to connect with you, not listen to a document being read aloud.
  • Over-relying on alcohol beforehand — One drink to steady the nerves is fine; more than that and the speech tends to ramble.
  • Mentioning exes or sensitive subjects — Even if intended as a joke, the risk isn't worth it.
  • Going on too long — The audience's attention will peak and then fade. End before you lose them.
  • Forgetting to thank people — The couple, the families, the venue, the guests — a brief thank-you covers a lot of goodwill.
  • Not practising the toast — The final moment should feel decisive and clear. Know exactly what your final line is.
