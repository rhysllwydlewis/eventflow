Event Insurance: What You Need to Know

Event insurance protects your investment if something goes wrong — whether it's a supplier cancellation, bad weather, or an unexpected illness. Here's what you need to know.

📚 6 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 15 March 2026

🛡️ Why Event Insurance Matters

Planning an event involves months of preparation and thousands of pounds in non-refundable deposits. Yet many people overlook insurance until something goes wrong — by which point it's too late. Event insurance is one of the most straightforward ways to protect your investment, and in most cases it's far more affordable than people expect.

Consider the risks you're exposed to without cover:

  • Cancellation due to illness: If you, a key family member, or a principal guest becomes seriously ill or passes away before the event, you may need to cancel or postpone. Without insurance, you could lose every deposit you've paid.
  • Supplier failure: If a supplier goes into administration — a photographer, caterer, or venue — you may lose your deposit and face emergency replacement costs at short notice.
  • Adverse weather: A flooded venue access road, a severe storm, or a heatwave that makes an outdoor venue unusable can force a cancellation. Certain weather events may be covered depending on your policy.
  • Venue closure: If your venue unexpectedly closes — due to fire damage, flooding, or insolvency — you'll need funds to find an alternative at the last minute.
  • Redundancy or job loss: Some policies include cover if you or your partner becomes redundant after booking and can no longer afford the event.

The UK events industry collectively loses millions of pounds each year to unforeseen cancellations and supplier failures. Event insurance exists precisely to prevent those losses falling entirely on the couple or event organiser.

📋 Types of Event Cover

Event insurance isn't a single product — it's a collection of cover types that you can mix and match depending on your needs and budget. Here's what each type covers:

Cancellation and Curtailment Cover

This is the core of any event insurance policy. It reimburses non-recoverable costs if you have to cancel or significantly cut short your event due to circumstances beyond your control — such as serious illness, bereavement, or a venue becoming unavailable. Most UK policies offer cancellation cover up to a specified limit, typically between £5,000 and £50,000.

Public Liability Insurance

Public liability cover protects you if a guest or third party suffers an injury or property damage at your event for which you're held responsible. This is particularly important if you're hiring a private venue or hosting an event in a space that doesn't include venue liability cover. Many venues will require you to have your own public liability insurance — especially for marquee events on private land.

Personal Accident Cover

This covers accidental injury to you or your partner in the lead-up to the event. Some policies extend this cover to members of the wedding party. It typically pays a lump sum in the event of serious injury that prevents attendance.

Marquee and Equipment Cover

If you're hiring a marquee, specialist lighting, audio equipment, or other high-value items, a standard event insurance policy may not cover accidental damage or theft. Marquee and equipment cover fills this gap. This is especially relevant for outdoor events where equipment is exposed to the elements overnight.

Supplier Failure Cover

Supplier failure cover — sometimes called "supplier insolvency cover" — reimburses deposits and additional costs if a booked supplier goes into administration or simply fails to turn up. This cover has become increasingly popular following several high-profile UK wedding company collapses in recent years. Always check whether your policy covers both insolvency and non-appearance, as these are sometimes treated separately.

🚫 What's Typically Excluded

Event insurance policies are not all-encompassing. Understanding what's excluded is just as important as knowing what's covered. Common exclusions include:

  • Pre-existing medical conditions: If you or a close family member has a known medical condition at the time of taking out the policy, claims arising from that condition are usually excluded. Always declare pre-existing conditions to your insurer — failing to do so can invalidate the entire policy.
  • Known circumstances: If you take out insurance after a problem has already arisen — for example, after your venue has announced financial difficulties — claims relating to that known issue will not be covered.
  • Disinclination to proceed: Simply changing your mind or deciding you no longer want to hold the event is not a valid reason to claim. Insurance covers unforeseen circumstances, not voluntary decisions.
  • Certain weather events: Policies vary widely on weather-related cancellations. General rain, cold weather, or typical British drizzle is rarely covered. Extreme weather events — such as a named storm or exceptional flooding — may be covered, but read the policy wording carefully.
  • Deliberate acts: Any cancellation or loss caused by a deliberate act on your part (or anyone acting on your behalf) will not be covered.
  • Pandemic-related cancellations: Following the COVID-19 pandemic, many insurers now explicitly exclude cancellations arising from communicable disease outbreaks, government-imposed restrictions, or epidemic/pandemic events. Check this carefully if it's a concern.

Important: Always read the policy wording in full before purchasing. If anything is unclear, contact the insurer and ask for written clarification. Don't rely on summary documents alone.

💷 How Much Does Event Insurance Cost?

Event insurance is considerably more affordable than most people assume. Prices vary depending on the type of event, the number of guests, the total value of costs being insured, and the level of cover you choose.

Typical UK Price Ranges

  • Small event (up to 50 guests, costs up to £5,000): From approximately £50–£80
  • Medium event (50–150 guests, costs up to £15,000): From approximately £80–£150
  • Large event (150+ guests, costs up to £30,000): From approximately £150–£250
  • Premium wedding cover (costs up to £50,000+): From approximately £200–£350

Adding extras such as marquee cover, supplier failure cover, or higher public liability limits will increase the premium. However, even a comprehensive policy with £50,000 of cancellation cover and £2 million public liability typically costs under £300 — a fraction of the deposits it protects.

Specialist vs General Insurers

Several UK insurers specialise in wedding and event insurance, including Dreamsaver, WeddingPlan Insurance, and Emerald Life. These specialist providers often offer more tailored cover and better claims handling than adding an event rider to a home insurance policy. It's worth comparing quotes from two or three providers before committing.

Tip: Check whether your existing home insurance policy includes any event cover before purchasing a standalone policy — some do include limited cover for hosted events.

📅 When to Buy Event Insurance

The short answer: as soon as you start paying deposits. Every pound you commit before you have insurance is a pound at risk.

In practice, this means taking out event insurance at the same time as — or ideally before — you pay your first deposit. For a wedding, that's often the venue deposit, which is frequently the largest single payment (sometimes £2,000–£5,000 or more).

Key Timing Considerations

  • Book as early as possible: Some insurers will not accept new policies within 14 to 30 days of the event date. If you leave it late, you may find yourself uninsurable.
  • Don't wait for a full guest list: You don't need to have every detail confirmed before purchasing. Most policies allow you to adjust the insured value as your costs grow.
  • Renew if you postpone: If your event is postponed for any reason, check whether your existing policy extends to the new date — or whether you need a new policy.

❓ Key Questions to Ask Your Insurer

Before purchasing a policy, get clear answers to the following:

  1. Does the policy cover supplier failure — including both insolvency and non-appearance?
  2. What is the maximum cancellation cover limit, and is it sufficient for my total event spend?
  3. Are pre-existing medical conditions excluded? If so, for whom?
  4. Does the policy include public liability, and what is the limit? (Aim for at least £2 million.)
  5. Does the policy cover adverse weather — and if so, what qualifies as covered weather?
  6. What is the excess (the amount I pay before the insurer contributes) on each type of claim?
  7. Is there cover for ceremonial attire, gifts, or rings?
  8. How do I make a claim, and what documentation will I need?
  9. Is the policy valid if my event is held outside the UK?
  10. Does the insurer have a 24-hour emergency helpline?
