Destination Wedding Planning: UK & Abroad

Whether you're dreaming of a Scottish castle, a Tuscan villa, or a sun-drenched Greek island, planning a destination wedding takes extra care — and extra organisation.

📚 10 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 11 March 2026

🌍 Choosing Your Destination

The first and most exciting decision is where to get married. Destination weddings fall into two broad categories: UK destinations and overseas locations. Both offer unique advantages, and the right choice depends on your guest list, budget, and personal vision.

UK Destination Weddings

The UK is remarkably varied. Scotland offers dramatic Highland scenery, ancient castles, and whisky distillery venues — all without passport complications. The Cotswolds provides quintessential English charm with honey-stone manor houses and rolling hills. Cornwall offers coastal beauty with dramatic clifftops and converted barns. Wales boasts castles, mountain backdrops, and excellent value compared to many English equivalents.

UK destination weddings are popular with couples who want a sense of occasion without the complexity of international travel. Guests with young children, elderly relatives, or limited budgets will also appreciate not needing flights or passports.

Overseas Destination Weddings

Europe is the most popular choice for UK couples heading abroad. Italy (Tuscany, the Amalfi Coast, Lake Como), France (Provence, the Dordogne), Spain (Catalonia, Andalusia), Greece (Santorini, Crete, Corfu), and Portugal (the Algarve, the Douro Valley) all offer reliable sunshine, stunning venues, and well-established wedding tourism industries.

Further afield, the Caribbean, Bali, Mauritius, and Sri Lanka appeal to couples wanting a truly exotic backdrop. Bear in mind that more distant locations typically mean higher costs for guests, and smaller attendance figures.

Key question: How important is it to you that all of your close family and friends attend? Longer distances and higher costs mean some guests may be unable to join you. Make this decision honestly before you fall in love with a specific venue.

⚖️ Legal Requirements

One of the most important — and frequently overlooked — aspects of a destination wedding is ensuring your marriage is legally valid. Requirements vary significantly depending on where you marry.

Getting Married in Scotland

Scotland has more flexible marriage laws than England and Wales, allowing outdoor ceremonies and a broader range of celebrants. You'll need to complete a Marriage Notice form at least 29 days before the ceremony, and both parties must provide their birth certificates and, if applicable, divorce or death certificates for previous marriages. A marriage schedule is issued in advance and must be signed at the ceremony.

Getting Married Abroad

If you marry in another country, that ceremony may or may not be automatically recognised in the UK. For many popular European destinations, a legal ceremony conducted locally is fully recognised. However, some couples choose to have a small legal ceremony in the UK first and then hold a symbolic celebration abroad — this simplifies the paperwork considerably.

Common documents required for overseas marriages include:

  • Certificate of No Impediment (CNI) — obtainable from the General Register Office or a local register office; confirms you are free to marry under UK law
  • Apostille — an official legalisation stamp applied to UK documents so they are accepted in other countries; arranged through the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO)
  • Birth certificates, passports, and proof of any prior divorces
  • Translated documents if the local authority requires them

Always contact the embassy or consulate of the destination country well in advance — ideally 12–18 months before — as requirements and processing times vary widely.

💰 Budgeting for a Destination Wedding

Destination weddings can be surprisingly cost-effective if you're planning a smaller, more intimate celebration. However, the additional logistics involved — travel, accommodation blocks, local suppliers — require careful financial planning.

What to Factor Into Your Budget

  • Venue hire: Some overseas venues include catering packages; others are dry hire. Understand what's included before comparing prices.
  • Travel and accommodation for the couple: Include reconnaissance trip costs (visiting the venue in person before booking is strongly recommended).
  • Supplier costs: Local suppliers may be cheaper than flying in UK-based ones, but verify quality carefully. Local wedding planners with good English are invaluable.
  • Legal fees: CNI, apostille, translation, and local registry fees can add up to several hundred pounds.
  • Guest accommodation block: Negotiating a room block at a nearby hotel for guests is helpful, even if you don't guarantee numbers.
  • Currency fluctuation: If paying overseas suppliers, exchange rate movements can affect your budget. Consider paying deposits in sterling where possible or using a currency hedging service.
  • Wedding insurance: Essential for any destination wedding — ensure your policy covers overseas ceremonies, supplier failure, and travel disruption.

📬 Communicating with Guests

Guest communication is more involved for a destination wedding. The more lead time and information you give people, the more likely they are to attend and the smoother the experience will be.

Save the Date Timing

Send save-the-dates at least 12 months in advance for an overseas destination — 18 months is even better for very far-flung locations. This gives guests maximum time to arrange flights, annual leave, accommodation, and budgets.

What to Include in Guest Communications

  • Nearest airports and recommended flight routes
  • Recommended accommodation options at a range of price points
  • Estimated all-in costs so guests can budget (flights, accommodation, transfers)
  • Visa requirements if applicable
  • Weather expectations and suggested dress code for the climate
  • A detailed day-by-day itinerary if guests are joining for multiple days
  • Local area guide — restaurants, things to do, tipping etiquette

A dedicated wedding website is particularly useful for destination weddings. It allows you to update information as logistics evolve and gives guests a single reference point. Include an FAQ section covering the most common queries.

🤝 Managing Suppliers Remotely

Working with suppliers you can't easily meet in person is one of the greatest challenges of a destination wedding. A local wedding planner or coordinator is worth every penny — they know the reliable local florists, caterers, and photographers, and they can manage last-minute issues on the day.

Finding Reputable Local Suppliers

  • Ask your venue for a recommended supplier list — established venues will have vetted reliable partners
  • Join destination-specific wedding forums and Facebook groups for first-hand recommendations
  • Look for suppliers with English-language reviews on international platforms
  • Use video calls (Zoom, FaceTime) for supplier meetings — treat them as seriously as in-person meetings
  • Visit in person at least once before the wedding, ideally 6–12 months out, to meet key suppliers face-to-face

Contracts and Payments

Always insist on a written contract, even if it's in a foreign language — have it translated. Understand the cancellation and postponement policy clearly before paying any deposit. Pay by credit card where possible for additional consumer protection. Never transfer large sums to suppliers you haven't thoroughly vetted.

Local planner tip: A good destination wedding planner will typically charge 10–15% of the total wedding budget. Given the complexity they manage, this is almost always money well spent.

📅 Day-of Logistics

With guests travelling from afar, day-of logistics require more planning than a local wedding. Think through every guest journey and make it as smooth as possible.

Transfers and Transport

Organising group transfers from accommodation to the venue — and back afterwards — removes a significant burden from guests. Consider minibuses or coaches for large groups, or negotiate rates with local taxi firms for smaller parties. Include transfer information in your final guest information pack.

A Welcome Event the Day Before

Many destination couples host an informal welcome dinner or drinks the evening before the wedding. This gives guests who've just arrived a chance to relax together, meets the couple in a lower-pressure setting, and makes the wedding day itself feel even more celebratory.

Emergency Planning

Always have a Plan B for weather-dependent elements. Know your venue's wet-weather contingency before you arrive. Ensure all suppliers have your local contact number. Designate a trusted friend or family member who can liaise with suppliers if you're occupied — or hire a day-of coordinator to manage everything on your behalf.
