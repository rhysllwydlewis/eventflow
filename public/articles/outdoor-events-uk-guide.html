Outdoor Events in the UK: Weatherproofing Your Plan

The UK's unpredictable weather is the elephant in the room at every outdoor event. Here's how to plan for the best and prepare for the worst.

📚 7 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 15 March 2026

🌦️ The UK Weather Reality

Let's be honest: the United Kingdom is not blessed with reliable summer weather. Even during the warmest months, a sudden downpour or gusty winds can derail an outdoor event that hasn't been properly planned. That said, the UK hosts thousands of successful outdoor events every year — the key is preparation, not pessimism.

Best Months for Outdoor Events

June, July, and August offer the highest average temperatures and longest daylight hours, making them the most popular months for outdoor events. May and September are also viable, with pleasant conditions on many days and fewer booking clashes than peak summer. April and October can work for hardier gatherings with robust shelter in place, but should be approached with more caution.

According to the Met Office, the UK's driest months on average are April, May, and June — though "dry" is relative, and any outdoor event planner should assume rain is possible regardless of the calendar date.

What to Expect

  • Average UK summer temperatures range from 17°C to 24°C — pleasant but rarely scorching.
  • Rain is possible on any day of the year, including in July and August.
  • Wind is a significant factor for marquees and temporary structures, particularly in coastal and exposed locations.
  • Evening temperatures can drop sharply, even in mid-summer — especially in Scotland and northern England.

📍 Choosing the Right Outdoor Venue

Not all outdoor spaces are created equal. The type of space you choose will significantly affect your contingency options and the permits you'll need.

Private Estates and Gardens

Hosting an event on private land — whether your own garden or a hired estate — gives you the most flexibility. You can generally erect a marquee, set up bespoke catering, and control the layout entirely. However, you'll need to check local planning rules for any temporary structures over a certain size, and you'll be responsible for obtaining any licences required.

Licensed Open Spaces and Parks

Many local councils license sections of parks and green spaces for private events. This can be a cost-effective option, but licences come with conditions: noise limits, permitted hours, no-dig rules for structures, and restrictions on alcohol. Apply well in advance — popular parks can be booked up a year or more ahead for summer dates.

Beaches

Beach events are romantic in concept but challenging in practice. You'll need permission from the relevant authority (the Crown Estate manages much of England's foreshore), and tidal patterns, sand, and salt air all create logistical complications for equipment and power.

Check early: Whatever outdoor space you're considering, contact the landowner or local authority as early as possible. Permits and permissions take time, and some spaces have limited availability or complex licensing requirements.

⛺ Marquees and Temporary Structures

A marquee is the most effective way to weatherproof an outdoor event. The right structure can keep guests comfortable regardless of what the sky decides to do.

Types of Marquee

  • Traditional pole marquees: The classic peaked canvas structure. Spacious and elegant, but requires guy ropes and significant ground space. Best suited to large, flat areas of grass.
  • Frame marquees: Self-supporting aluminium frames with no internal poles, making the most of usable floor space. Can be erected on harder surfaces including patios and car parks.
  • Stretch tents: Modern, sculptural fabric structures that can be configured in a variety of shapes. Highly versatile and visually striking, but may offer less protection in heavy rain than a traditional marquee.
  • Tipis and yurts: Popular for bohemian-style weddings. Typically require linking together for larger guest numbers, and the canvas walls are not always fully weatherproof in driving rain.
  • Clearspan structures: Industrial-grade modular buildings that resemble temporary halls. Used for large corporate events or festivals; offer the most robust weather protection.

Ground Conditions and Flooring

Ground conditions matter enormously. A wet summer can turn a grass field into a muddy quagmire within hours of guests arriving. Ask your marquee supplier about flooring options: interlocking wooden boards (the most popular choice for weddings), matting, or raised flooring systems. For events over soft ground, ensure the supplier stakes the marquee correctly and that drainage is considered.

Heating and Lighting

Even in July, evening temperatures in a marquee can become uncomfortably cool. Budget for heating — either industrial propane heaters or, for a more elegant look, festoon lighting combined with smaller radiant heaters placed around the perimeter. All heating equipment must be correctly installed and PAT tested.

🌧️ Wet Weather Contingency Plans

Every outdoor event needs a documented wet weather plan — not as a pessimistic afterthought, but as a structured contingency that you can activate quickly if needed.

  • Wet-weather gazebos: For events without a full marquee, a series of linked gazebos can provide emergency overhead cover. Ensure they are properly weighted or staked — freestanding gazebos can become dangerous in even moderate wind.
  • Floor covering: Pre-lay outdoor matting or roll-out carpet on grass walkways between key areas (car park, entrance, marquee, toilet block). This prevents guests sinking into mud and keeps shoes clean.
  • Drainage: If your venue has known drainage issues, flag this with your supplier early. Sandbags, sump pumps, and French drains can all be arranged in advance for high-risk sites.
  • Indoor fallback: If the venue has any indoor space at all — a barn, a converted outbuilding, a village hall — establish in advance whether it can be used as an emergency fallback and what its capacity is.
  • Communication plan: Decide in advance at what point you will activate your wet weather plan, and how you'll communicate the change to guests. A group WhatsApp message or email sent the morning of the event is entirely normal for outdoor events.

👥 Guest Comfort

Comfortable guests are happy guests. Outdoor events in the UK require some additional thought around comfort that indoor venues handle automatically.

  • Shade: On hot, sunny days — yes, they do happen — shade is as important as shelter from rain. Parasols, shade sails, and trees all help. Avoid seating elderly guests in direct full sun for extended periods.
  • Heating: As noted above, plan for evening temperatures to drop. Provide outdoor heaters and, if your event extends into the night, consider having a supply of blankets available.
  • Lighting: Once the sun sets, outdoor spaces become dark quickly. Plan your lighting carefully: festoon lights for atmosphere, pathway lighting for safety, and clear signage to guide guests between areas.
  • Wellies welcome: If your venue involves any walking across grass, include a note in your invitations suggesting appropriate footwear. Many guests will appreciate it — and you'll avoid the awkward sight of stiletto heels disappearing into soft ground.
  • Seating: Ensure there is always sufficient seating for all guests, including older attendees who cannot stand for extended periods. Hay bales and picnic blankets are charming but impractical for less mobile guests.
  • Insect management: In warm, humid conditions — particularly near water — insects can be a nuisance. Citronella candles, subtle repellent diffusers, and avoiding food left uncovered can all help.

📜 Permits and Licences

Outdoor events in the UK are subject to a range of potential legal requirements. The specific permits you need depend on the nature of your event and the venue.

Temporary Event Notice (TEN)

If your outdoor event involves the sale or supply of alcohol, provision of regulated entertainment (live music, recorded music for dancing), or late-night refreshments between 11pm and 5am, you may need a Temporary Event Notice. These are applied for through your local council and typically cost around £21 each. There are limits on the number of TENs per premises and per person per year, so check requirements early.

Noise

Outdoor amplified music carries much further than indoor sound. Most local authorities will investigate noise complaints at outdoor events, and some areas have specific noise level restrictions. Speak to your local Environmental Health team before the event if you're planning amplified music after 9pm — they can advise on acceptable levels and help you avoid a complaint on the day.

Planning Permission for Structures

Temporary structures (marquees) are generally exempt from planning permission if they are up for fewer than 28 days in a calendar year and are not on certain protected land. However, this varies, and you should confirm with your local planning authority if you are unsure.

🔧 Supplier Considerations for Outdoor Events

Not all suppliers are equally comfortable working in outdoor environments. When hiring for an outdoor event, raise the following with each supplier:

  • Power supply: Is there mains power on site, or will a generator be required? Who supplies and operates the generator? Ensure your caterer, band, and lighting supplier all have their power requirements confirmed and coordinated.
  • Access: Can large vehicles — catering vans, marquee lorries, mobile bars — access the site? Check ground conditions, track widths, and any weight restrictions on access routes.
  • Weather cancellation clauses: Ask each supplier what their policy is if the event has to be cancelled or significantly altered due to extreme weather. Ensure this is documented in their contract.
  • Equipment suitability: DJ and band equipment needs to be kept dry. Ensure your marquee provides adequate cover over the performance area, and that there is a plan for moving sensitive equipment if rain begins unexpectedly.
