Event Photography: Shot List Template

A well-planned shot list is the secret to great event photography. Here's how to create one — plus a template you can use and adapt for your own event.

📚 5 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 15 March 2026

📸 Why a Shot List Matters

Your event photographer is talented — but they're not a mind reader. Without a shot list, even the most experienced photographer can miss a moment that matters deeply to you: a quiet word between a parent and child, a specific group of friends who flew in from abroad, the intricate detail on your cake. A shot list ensures that nothing important is overlooked.

A good shot list serves three key purposes:

  • It ensures nothing is missed. Events move quickly. With dozens of things happening simultaneously, a written list provides a clear reference that both you and your photographer can check against throughout the day.
  • It helps your photographer plan their time. Knowing in advance that you want 15 minutes for family formals, or that there's a particular view at the venue you'd love to use at golden hour, lets your photographer structure their workflow and position themselves in the right place at the right time.
  • It gives you peace of mind. Once the shot list is agreed, you can focus on enjoying your event rather than mentally chasing your photographer around to ask for specific shots.

Important: A shot list is a guide, not a script. Share it with your photographer at least two to four weeks before the event so they can flag anything impractical and suggest alternatives.

🌅 Before the Event: Preparation Shots

Some of the most treasured event photographs are taken before the main proceedings begin. Don't underestimate the value of the preparation and detail shots — they tell the story of the day from the very start.

Venue Details

  • Exterior of the venue, including any signage or entrance features
  • Ceremony or event space before guests arrive (chairs, flowers, table settings)
  • Close-up of table decorations, centrepieces, and place cards
  • Ceremony arch, aisle decorations, or stage setup
  • Venue overview shot from a high vantage point, if available

Stationery and Décor

  • Invitations, order of service, or event programmes
  • Seating plan and table names or numbers
  • Favours and gifts for guests
  • Menus and name cards
  • Cake table and any other food or drink displays

Getting-Ready Shots (Weddings and Formal Events)

  • The dress or outfit hanging up
  • Shoes, jewellery, and accessories laid out
  • Bouquet or buttonhole close-up
  • Getting dressed / final preparations
  • Candid moments with family or bridal party during preparation
  • A portrait before leaving for the venue

🎯 Key Moment Shots

These are the headline shots — the moments that will define your event's visual story. Adapt this list to suit your specific event type.

Weddings

  • Guests arriving and taking their seats
  • Wedding party processional
  • Couple's first look at the altar
  • Exchange of vows and rings
  • The kiss
  • Signing of the register
  • Recessional (walking back down the aisle as a married couple)
  • Confetti moment
  • Couple portraits (golden hour or at a chosen location)
  • First dance
  • Speeches (speaker and reactions from guests)
  • Cake cutting
  • Bouquet toss
  • Evening dancing and atmosphere shots

Corporate Events

  • Guests arriving and registering
  • Keynote speaker at the podium
  • Audience reactions during presentations
  • Panel discussions or Q&A sessions
  • Networking moments
  • Award presentations (recipient receiving the award, shaking hands)
  • Brand and sponsor signage
  • Group photographs of teams or departments

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Group and Family Portrait Shots

Family formals — the posed group photographs — are often the most logistically challenging part of the day. A clear list and an efficient system can turn a potentially chaotic 40-minute exercise into a smooth 15-minute photo session.

Organising Groups Efficiently

The key to quick family formals is starting with the largest groups and working down to smaller ones. This means you release more people earlier and avoid keeping everyone waiting. Number your groups on the shot list and brief a family member or friend in advance to help gather people.

Example group photo order for a wedding:

  1. Full wedding party (both families together)
  2. Couple with both sets of parents
  3. Couple with bride's family
  4. Couple with groom's family
  5. Couple with grandparents
  6. Couple with siblings
  7. Couple with bridesmaids
  8. Couple with groomsmen
  9. Full wedding party (bridesmaids and groomsmen)
  10. Couple alone

Pro tip: Limit your formal group shots to 10–12 combinations maximum. Any more than this and guests become restless, light changes, and the relaxed atmosphere you're trying to capture starts to drain away.

🎉 Candid and Atmospheric Shots

The candid photographs — guests laughing, children dancing, quiet moments between friends — are often the images people treasure most. These can't be scripted, but you can encourage them by letting your photographer know what matters to you.

  • Guests laughing and interacting during the drinks reception
  • Children playing or dancing
  • Elderly relatives watching the proceedings
  • Guests reading the order of service or event programme
  • Emotional reactions during speeches or ceremony
  • Detail shots: food being served, glasses being raised, hands being held
  • The dance floor from a high angle, showing the full crowd
  • Atmospheric shots of the venue as light changes through the day
  • Any specific guests you particularly want photographed (note names on the list)

📋 Shot List Template

Copy and adapt the structure below for your own event. Share it with your photographer as a Word document, PDF, or even a shared Google Doc so they can annotate it with notes.

EVENT PHOTOGRAPHY SHOT LIST

Event: [Event name and type]

Date: [Date]

Venue: [Venue name and address]

Photographer: [Name]

BEFORE THE EVENT

  • Venue exterior and entrance
  • Room setup and décor details
  • Table settings close-ups
  • Stationery flat lay
  • [Getting ready shots — if applicable]

KEY MOMENTS

  • Guests arriving
  • [Ceremony / keynote / main event moments]
  • [Specific milestone moments relevant to your event]

GROUP PHOTOGRAPHS

  • Group 1: [Names]
  • Group 2: [Names]
  • Group 3: [Names]
  • [Continue as needed]

CANDID AND ATMOSPHERE

  • Guests mingling during reception
  • Specific guests to capture: [Names]
  • Food and drink presentation
  • Evening atmosphere

NOTES FOR PHOTOGRAPHER

  • [Any special requests, sensitivities, or timing notes]
  • [Location of golden-hour portrait spot]
  • [Any guests who prefer not to be photographed]

🤝 Working With Your Photographer

A shot list is a collaboration, not a set of demands. The best results come from open communication between you and your photographer well before the day itself.

Sharing the List

Share your draft shot list at your pre-event meeting or planning call — ideally four to six weeks before the event. Give your photographer time to review it, ask questions, and flag any shots that may be impractical given the venue layout, schedule, or light conditions.

When to Be Flexible

If your photographer suggests adjusting the order of group shots, or recommends a different location for couple portraits, trust their expertise. They've likely worked at your venue before and know which spots photograph well and which look better in person than in print. The shot list is a starting point — the best photographers use it as a framework and then respond creatively to what unfolds on the day.

Equally, if a spontaneous moment presents itself — an unplanned first dance, a surprise guest's reaction, a beautiful shaft of light through a window — give your photographer the freedom to capture it. Some of the best event photographs are the ones nobody planned.
