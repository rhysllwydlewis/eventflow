Guest List Management: The Complete Guide

From your first draft to the final seating plan — everything you need to manage your guest list with confidence

📚 9 min read • Updated March 2026

✍️ EventFlow Team 📅 Published: 6 March 2026

📝 Starting Your Guest List

The guest list is one of the first — and most emotionally charged — decisions you'll make as you plan your event. For weddings especially, it can quickly become a source of stress as family expectations, venue capacity, and budget collide.

Start by creating an unconstrained initial list. Write down everyone you'd ideally invite without worrying about numbers or cost. Once you have that list, you can start to work backwards from your venue capacity and per-head catering budget to arrive at your final number.

The A-List and B-List Approach

A practical strategy used by many couples and event planners is to split your initial list into two tiers:

  • A-list: People you absolutely must invite — close family and best friends. These guests receive invitations first.
  • B-list: People you'd love to include if numbers allow — extended family, work colleagues, old friends. These guests are invited as space opens up from A-list declines.

The key is to send B-list invitations early enough that those guests don't feel like an afterthought. Aim to contact them no later than 8 weeks before the event.

Important: Never mention to B-list guests that they were on a secondary list. Simply extend a warm and genuine invitation.

Setting Your Number

Work with these key constraints to arrive at your guest count:

  1. Confirm your venue's maximum ceremony and reception capacity
  2. Divide your catering budget by your caterer's per-head price
  3. Check whether your venue has a minimum spend or minimum numbers clause
  4. Factor in children — many venues count under-12s at half the adult rate, or even free

👨‍👩‍👧 Managing Plus Ones and Children

Plus Ones

Deciding your plus-one policy early avoids awkward conversations later. Common approaches include:

  • Established couples only: Invite both halves of any couple who live together or have been together for 12+ months.
  • Wedding party only: Restrict plus ones to your immediate wedding party to keep numbers manageable.
  • No plus ones: Acceptable for smaller, intimate events — be consistent and apply this rule to everyone.

Whatever policy you choose, be consistent. Offering plus ones to some single friends but not others is a reliable way to cause hurt feelings.

Children at Your Event

The decision to have a child-free event is perfectly valid, but communicate it clearly and early so parents can make childcare arrangements. If you are inviting children, consider:

  • Providing a dedicated children's area with activities, colouring, and games
  • Offering a children's menu (simpler, more affordable dishes)
  • Hiring a registered childminder for the evening so parents can relax
  • Specifying on the invitation whether children are welcome at the ceremony, wedding breakfast, evening reception, or all three

🍽️ Collecting Dietary Requirements

Gathering dietary information from guests is essential — and easier when you build it into your RSVP process rather than chasing people afterwards.

What to Ask

  • Vegetarian or vegan
  • Food allergies (the 14 major allergens as required under UK food law: celery, cereals containing gluten, crustaceans, eggs, fish, lupin, milk, molluscs, mustard, peanuts, sesame, soybeans, sulphur dioxide, and tree nuts)
  • Religious dietary requirements (halal, kosher, Jain vegetarian, Sattvic vegetarian)
  • Other intolerances or strong dislikes

Passing Information to Your Caterer

Compile dietary information into a clear spreadsheet and share it with your caterer at least four weeks before the event. Include the guest's name, table number, and specific requirements for each person. For serious allergies, flag these separately and follow up with your caterer to confirm how they will be handled in the kitchen.

Allergen responsibility: Under UK law, caterers must be able to provide full allergen information for every dish. Confirm this in writing with your caterer before signing the contract.

📬 RSVP Management

Chasing RSVPs is one of the most time-consuming parts of event planning. A structured approach makes it significantly easier.

Setting Your RSVP Deadline

For weddings, set your RSVP deadline 6–8 weeks before the event. This gives you time to finalise catering numbers, complete the seating plan, and chase non-responders. Set a firm deadline on the invitation and stick to it.

RSVP Methods

  • Digital RSVP: A wedding website with an integrated RSVP form is the most efficient method. Guests can respond instantly and their dietary requirements are automatically recorded.
  • RSVP card: Include a pre-addressed, stamped reply card with the invitation. Traditional and easy for guests of all ages.
  • Email: Works well for corporate events and informal gatherings.

Chasing Non-Responders

Send a polite reminder one week after your deadline to everyone who hasn't responded. After a second reminder, it's acceptable to assume non-response means they cannot attend — but always phone close family and friends directly rather than leaving it to chance.

Day Guests vs Evening Guests

For weddings, you'll often have two separate guest lists: all-day guests (ceremony, wedding breakfast, evening) and evening-only guests. Manage these as separate lists with different RSVP deadlines and invitation wording. Evening guests typically receive invitations four to six weeks before the wedding rather than the full twelve weeks.

💺 Seating Arrangements

A well-considered seating plan makes a significant difference to the atmosphere at your wedding breakfast or corporate dinner.

General Principles

  • Keep families together where possible, but mix friendship groups to encourage conversation
  • Place guests with mobility difficulties or hearing impairments where they have the best access and acoustics
  • Seat children near the edge of the room so parents can exit easily if needed
  • Consider the dynamics of divorced or estranged family members — seat them at opposite ends of the room with plenty of buffer guests
  • Put single guests on tables with other singles or especially sociable couples

Table Plan Display

Display your table plan prominently at the entrance to the dining room so guests can find their table before the meal. Alphabetical ordering by surname is the easiest for guests to navigate. Individual place cards on each chair make it clear exactly where guests should sit.

⚠️ Handling Awkward Situations

Uninvited Guests

It's rare but it does happen: someone turns up at your event who wasn't on the list. The best approach is to have a designated person — your wedding co-ordinator, a trusted friend, or a venue manager — who can handle gate-crashing diplomatically, without involving you on the day.

Last-Minute Cancellations

Inform your caterer immediately of any cancellations received within the final two weeks. Most caterers have a cut-off date after which the full per-head charge still applies regardless of attendance. Check this clause carefully in your catering contract.

Unexpected Additions

If a guest turns up with an uninvited plus one, the venue and catering team will usually do their best to accommodate them if space allows. Always have a small buffer built into your final catering numbers — many caterers recommend over-ordering by 5% for exactly this reason.
